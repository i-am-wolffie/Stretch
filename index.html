Daily Stretch Routine

18 stretches · ~22 min · Post-gym & post-desk

Post-gym
Post-desk
Plantar fasciitis
Phase 1 — Standing
1
Standing Chest Opener
Pectorals · Anterior deltoid · Thoracic extension
Desk
30–45s × 3

Stand tall. Clasp hands behind your back, squeeze shoulder blades together, and lift your arms slightly while opening the chest upward. Hold 30–45 seconds. Release. Repeat 3 times.

Why: Desk sitting drives the shoulders forward and collapses the chest into flexion all day. This reverses that pattern.
PectoralsAnterior deltoidThoracic extension
2
Doorway Chest Stretch
Pectorals · Anterior capsule · Internal rotation correction
GymDesk
30s each side × 2

Place forearm against a wall or doorframe at 90°, elbow at shoulder height. Step forward until you feel a strong stretch across the chest and front of shoulder. Vary elbow height for different fibres.

Why: Presses and rows create internal rotation. Sitting compounds it. This directly counteracts that pattern and restores shoulder joint centration.
PectoralsAnterior capsuleShoulder rotation
3
Standing Hip Flexor Lunge
Iliopsoas · Rectus femoris · Anterior pelvis
GymDesk
45–60s each side

Step forward into a deep lunge. Back knee can touch the floor. Drive the hips forward and squeeze the glute of the back leg. Keep the torso tall. Raise the same-side arm overhead for more stretch.

Why: Hip flexors shortened by both sitting and squatting. A tight iliopsoas anteriorly tilts the pelvis and causes lower back pain. The glute squeeze deepens the stretch via reciprocal inhibition.
IliopsoasRectus femorisLower back
4
Standing Lat Stretch
Latissimus dorsi · Teres major · Thoracolumbar fascia
Gym
30s each side × 2

Stand next to a doorframe. Reach overhead with one hand and grip it. Lean your body away, letting your arm pull the lat into a long stretch. Hold 30 seconds each side.

Why: After pulling work, the lats pull the shoulders into internal rotation. Commonly missed in gym routines.
Latissimus dorsiTeres majorShoulder rotation
5
Standing Calf & Achilles
Gastrocnemius · Soleus · Achilles tendon
Gym
45s each position, each leg

Gastrocnemius (straight knee): Step back into a lunge, keep the back leg straight, press the heel flat. 45 seconds.

Soleus/Achilles (bent knee): Same position, bend the back knee toward the floor while keeping the heel down. 45 seconds.

Why: Tight calves alter gait mechanics, increase Achilles stress, and contribute to plantar fasciitis. Two-position stretch is essential to cover both muscles.
GastrocnemiusSoleusAchillesAnkle mobility
6
Standing Forward Fold
Hamstrings · Lumbar erectors · Thoracic spine
GymDesk
60s

Stand with feet hip-width. Hinge at the hips and hang forward, letting gravity do the work. Let your head drop fully. Soft bend in knees is fine. Optional: clasp opposite elbows and sway gently.

Why: Gravity traction is more effective than forced holds. The 60s hold gives the nervous system time to release, not just the muscle.
HamstringsLumbar erectorsSpinal decompression
Phase 2 — Floor (stay down until done)
7
Thoracic Extension Over Rolled Towel
Thoracic spine · Intercostals · Kyphosis reversal
Desk
2–3 min

Roll a towel into a firm cylinder. Lie back over it so it sits across your mid-back at the thoracic spine level (between shoulder blades). Arms can cross the chest or extend overhead. Let gravity work. Breathe deeply.

Why: Sitting collapses the thoracic spine into flexion for hours. This is the single most important desk recovery exercise. Two to three minutes is needed for the connective tissue to respond.
Thoracic spineIntercostalsPosture
8
Thread the Needle
Thoracic rotation · Posterior capsule · Rhomboids
Desk
45s each side × 2

Start on all fours. Slide one arm under your body, threading it through until your shoulder and temple rest on the floor. The top arm can reach opposite for more rotation. Hold 45 seconds each side. Repeat twice.

Why: The thoracic spine loses rotational mobility quickly from sustained sitting. This targets the mid-back specifically where rotation is anatomically intended but frequently locked.
Thoracic rotationPosterior capsuleRhomboids
9
Child's Pose with Lat Reach
Lats · Thoracolumbar fascia · Hip flexors
GymDesk
60s centre + 30s each side

Kneel and sit back onto heels. Walk hands forward until fully extended. Let your chest sink toward the floor. 60 seconds centre. Then walk both hands to the right — feel the left lat lengthen. 30 seconds. Repeat left.

Why: Simultaneously loads lats, thoracolumbar fascia, and hip flexors. The lateral reach adds specific lat bias the central position doesn't provide.
LatsThoracolumbar fasciaHip flexors
10
Figure-Four / Piriformis Stretch
Piriformis · Glute medius · Sciatic nerve
GymDesk
60s each side

Lie on your back, knees bent. Cross the right ankle over the left knee. Flex the right foot (pull toes toward shin) to protect the knee. Draw both legs toward your chest or press the right knee gently away.

If you feel this in the knee rather than the glute, re-flex the foot or adjust the angle.

Why: A tight piriformis is one of the most common causes of lower back and sciatic-type pain — compressed by both sitting and squatting.
PiriformisGlute mediusSciatic nerve
11
Supine Glute Stretch
Gluteus maximus · Lumbar extensors · Sacroiliac joint
GymDesk
45s each side, then both

Lie on your back. Pull one knee to your chest, holding the shin or back of the thigh. Keep the opposite leg flat on the floor. Hold 45 seconds each side. Then draw both knees to chest and rock gently for 30 seconds.

Why: The gluteus maximus is the largest and most trained muscle yet almost never stretched. Glute tightness pulls on the sacrum and contributes to lower back stiffness.
Gluteus maximusLumbar extensorsSacroiliac joint
12
Supine Hamstring Stretch
Hamstrings · Sciatic nerve · Posterior chain
GymDesk
60s each side

Lie on your back. Raise one leg and hold behind the thigh or calf. Straighten the leg as far as comfortable, toes pointing toward the ceiling. Keep the other leg flat. Use a towel or belt if needed.

Why: The supine position stabilises the pelvis, preventing the lumbar spine from compensating. A 60-second hold is needed for viscoelastic relaxation — shorter holds are insufficient.
HamstringsSciatic nervePosterior chain
13
Kneeling Quad & Tucked-Toe
Quadriceps · Rectus femoris · Plantar fascia
Gym
30s tucked + 45s flat

Phase 1 — Toes tucked (30s): Kneel with toes curled under. Sit back gently onto your heels — strong stretch along the sole of the foot and plantar fascia.

Phase 2 — Toes flat, lean back (45s): Straighten toes flat. Place hands behind you and lean back to stretch the quad.

Why: Two-phase efficiency — tucked toe is one of the most effective plantar fascia stretches. The lean-back is superior to standing quad stretches because both knees load simultaneously under bodyweight gravity.
QuadricepsPlantar fasciaAchilles
14
Butterfly / Groin Stretch
Hip adductors · Groin · Medial knee
GymDesk
60–90s

Sit on the floor. Bring the soles of your feet together. Bring your feet closer toward your groin to increase the stretch. Sit tall. Allow gravity to lower the knees. For more depth, gently press knees down with elbows.

Why: Bringing the feet closer widens the knee angle which creates the adductor stretch — the common error of moving feet away is counterproductive.
Hip adductorsGroinInner thigh
15
Seated Spinal Twist
Thoracic rotation · Lumbar rotators · IT band
Desk
45s each side

Sit on the floor, legs extended. Bend one knee and place that foot outside the opposite knee. Rotate toward the bent knee, placing the opposite elbow on the outside of it. Use the elbow as a lever. Sit tall.

Why: This works primarily at the thoracic spine and hips, exactly where desk workers lose rotation. The IT band also receives beneficial tensioning.
Thoracic rotationLumbar rotatorsIT band
16
Suboccipital Release
Suboccipitals · Upper cervical · Tension headache prevention
Desk
2–3 min

Lie on your back. Place both thumbs (or a rolled towel) at the base of your skull, just below the occipital ridge. Let the weight of your head create gentle traction. Relax fully — don't push. Add small yes/no head nods to mobilise C1/C2.

Why: Screen work shortens the four suboccipital muscles — the primary cause of tension headaches and neck stiffness. Most PTIs miss this entirely.
SuboccipitalsUpper cervicalTension headaches
Plantar Fasciitis Treatment
Note: Do stretch 17 every morning before your first step out of bed — not just in this routine. That single habit is the most evidence-backed intervention for plantar fasciitis.
17
Seated Plantar Fascia Stretch
Plantar fascia · Toe extensors · First-step pain
PF
30s × 3 each foot

Sit in a chair or on the floor. Cross your right foot over your left knee. Grip your toes and pull them firmly back toward your shin until you feel a strong stretch along the arch. Hold 30 seconds. Repeat 3 times each foot.

First thing in the morning: Do this before taking your first step out of bed. The plantar fascia tightens overnight and the first step tears the fibres slightly — pre-stretching prevents that micro-damage.

Why: The most clinically validated stretch for plantar fasciitis. The 3 × 30s protocol is specifically what the research uses. Do it here AND every morning before standing.
Plantar fasciaArchFirst-step pain
18
Frozen Bottle Roll
Plantar fascia · Arch massage · Anti-inflammatory
PF
2 min each foot

Freeze a standard plastic water bottle. Place it on the floor. Sit in a chair and slowly roll the sole of your foot from heel to ball and back, applying moderate downward pressure. 2 minutes each foot. Focus extra time on tender spots.

Why: Combines mechanical stretching with anti-inflammatory cold therapy simultaneously. Particularly effective immediately after activity.
Plantar fasciaArchHeel insertion
Senior PT Note: This routine addresses every major tightness pattern for someone training at the gym and sitting at a desk. Never bounce a stretch. A strong pull is correct; sharp or joint pain means stop. Consistent use 5–7 nights per week will produce noticeable ROM improvements within 3–4 weeks. The plantar fasciitis protocol requires both the morning stretch and the evening routine — evenings alone won't resolve it.
